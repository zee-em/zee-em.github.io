text_ocean

An experiment in random access reading and text visualization, using Herman Melville's notoriously impenetrable whalefishery epic 'Moby Dick' as source material.

Selections of the text are blown apart and become a dynamic sea of words, animated according to grammatical function. The user explores the text by diving into the depths or surfacing, using the up and down arrow keys. The user "reads" by clicking to hook an intriguing word as it drifts or darts past. When a word is hooked, the other words belonging to its line are also slowly pulled in, and assemble, if reluctantly, to be read. Pressing "return" will release the words into the sea, but each time a word is unhooked, its connection to its original line of text may be lost. A wild unhooked word may find another line to swim with; the text is slowly, randomly rewritten by the user, word by word, as it is read. This work was made in Processing, and is optimized for Google Chrome. Commissioned by Turbulence/New Radio. Experience text_ocean on the Turbulence Archive.

moodMixer

As part of an internship at Microsoft Research, I prototyped a visual and emotional interface and player for personal music libraries using Processing and Java..

The project scraped crowdsourced user generated “social tags” from Last.fm and used keyword marching to assign an emotional quality or “mood” category (ie, chill, hyper, gloomy, romantic, melancholy, etc) to each song in a library, regardless of genre. Users populate their playlist by clicking on the color-coded mood buttons, the playlist can be shuffled and adjusted, and the categories can be reassigned. The social tags from Last.fm user data for the current song scroll across the top of the player as that song plays. Created in 2009, moodMixer was designed as a lightweight, fun, colorful, visual, and emotional alternative to text-heavy music players of the era. This video demonstrates the project; note that there is no sound in the video until 00:40.
